Chapter Five
When Power and Data Share the Same Fabric

Every autonomous device in the physical world needs two things: power and communication.

The history of wireless technology treats these as separate problems. The separation is not fundamental. It is historical. The Unified Energy‑Data Mesh is the formal recognition that the Meridian mesh already routes data — and the extension to carry both resources under one protocol.

Abstract

The Meridian mesh already carries two kinds of traffic: RF energy bursts and ~11 KB/s of structured, addressed control‑plane data per node. This paper formalizes the co‑routing architecture of the Unified Energy‑Data Mesh (UEDM) — the simultaneous routing of energy and data through the same physical node infrastructure under a shared addressing and governance framework.

I define the co‑routing arbitration rules, the UEDM addressing scheme (unified 128‑bit namespace), and the self‑sustaining device (SSD) model — autonomous sensors requiring no battery and no separate communication radio.

4. UEDM Architecture

Architectural Principle: A joule and a bit are both payloads; the difference is their physical carrier, not their addressing, routing, or governance.

UEDM Frame Header (unified for energy and data):
  src_node_id     : 128 bits (UEDM EIP address)
  dst_node_id     : 128 bits (UEDM EIP address)
  resource_type   : 4 bits   (ENERGY=0x1, DATA=0x2, MIXED=0x3, CONTROL=0x4)
  priority_class  : 4 bits   (CRITICAL / HIGH / NORMAL / BACKGROUND)
  seq_num         : 32 bits
  path_id         : 32 bits
  timing_window   : 128 bits
  safety_flags    : 16 bits  (BSL status, ENERGY type only)
  payload_length  : 16 bits
  checksum        : 32 bits
  [payload]       : variable

5. Co‑Routing Arbitration

Three Capacity Pools

  • Pool A — Guaranteed Energy Capacity (GEC): 60–90% of total frame capacity. Reserved for CRITICAL and HIGH energy sessions.
  • Pool B — Guaranteed Control Data Capacity (GCDC): 5–10%. Reserved for essential control‑plane data.
  • Pool C — Flexible Shared Capacity (FSC): Remainder. Shared between NORMAL/BACKGROUND energy and application data.

Arbitration Rules

RuleDescription
AR‑01GEC reserved exclusively for CRITICAL + HIGH energy
AR‑02GCDC reserved for essential control‑plane data
AR‑03NORMAL energy > all data in FSC
AR‑04BACKGROUND energy yields to NORMAL data in FSC
AR‑05CRITICAL data served from GCDC, protected
AR‑07Mode 2 (SWIPT) used when beam/data paths overlap
AR‑08Large data payloads fragmented across FSC slots

6. UEDM Addressing

Service Endpoint:
  [UEDM Address : 128 bits] + [Service Port : 16 bits] = 144 bits

Reserved Ports:
  0x0001 : Energy delivery (EaaS)
  0x0002 : Guardian Security authentication
  0x0003 : Lume-X telemetry
  0x0004 : SHDCL recovery messaging
  0x0005 : EDNS query/response
  0x0006 : EBGP peering session
  0x0100–0xFFFF : Application-defined services

7. The Self‑Sustaining Device Model

A self‑sustaining device (SSD) receives all operating power and conducts all data communication through the UEDM. No battery. No separate radio. No external infrastructure dependency beyond mesh coverage.

SSD Operating Budget (10 mW received power):
  Microcontroller (Cortex-M4 at 4 MHz)    : 1.2 mW
  Sensor (temperature + humidity, 1 Hz)   : 0.8 mW
  RF transceiver (receive mode)           : 2.0 mW
  RF transceiver (transmit mode, 1% duty) : 0.5 mW avg
  Energy management circuit               : 0.3 mW
  Margin for supercapacitor charging      : 5.2 mW

10. Applications

  • Smart Building: Every device — occupancy sensors, HVAC controls, security cameras — is an SSD. Installation is physical mounting only; no wiring required.
  • Industrial IoT: Manufacturing facilities with thousands of sensors powered and connected through one mesh. The manufacturing DPI uses UEDM as its resource routing fabric.
  • Emergency Response: Portable UEDM anchor nodes create instant infrastructure for emergency responders — power and communication simultaneously.
  • Agricultural Monitoring: Dense sensor networks across large areas, solar‑powered anchor nodes on poles, relay chains extending coverage.

Part Three Closing

Chapters Four and Five have traced Meridian from a single building mesh to a global energy internet to a unified infrastructure that routes both energy and data. The protocol stack is complete. The addressing is defined. The self‑sustaining device is characterized.

What is not yet specified is how this infrastructure defends itself. Part Four specifies the security architecture.
