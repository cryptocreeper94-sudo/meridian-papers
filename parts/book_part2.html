Chapter One
The Four‑Layer Architecture

Every great infrastructure technology is, at its core, an abstraction. The electrical grid abstracts away the complexity of power generation. The internet abstracts away the complexity of network topology. Meridian is an abstraction for wireless energy delivery. It separates the “what” — deterministic, loss‑bounded, addressed energy delivery to any device in the network — from the “how” — the ambient harvesting, burst‑mode storage, multi‑hop relay, and directed transmission mechanisms that implement the delivery.

Abstract

We present Meridian, a four‑layer deterministic wireless energy routing architecture that unifies ambient energy harvesting, burst‑mode storage, mesh‑based relay coordination, and directed wireless power delivery under a single governing framework. Meridian defines a universal energy coordinate system, a deterministic flow engine, a multi‑hop mesh fabric, and a directional transmission layer — designated Meridian Core (MC), Meridian Flow Engine (MFE), Meridian Mesh Fabric (MMF), and Meridian Transmission Layer (MTL) respectively.

Meridian integrates three established DarkWave subsystems: DWER (Deterministic Wireless Energy Routing) for point‑to‑point directed beam delivery, DRMA (Distributed Relay Mesh Architecture) for multi‑hop energy routing, and DAEH (Deterministic Ambient Energy Harvesting) for node‑autonomous power accumulation. The architecture is controlled by Lume‑X, a provisionally patented deterministic control runtime validated at 73 Hz cycle frequency, and enforced by Guardian Security (energy‑domain designation: Guardian‑E).

No experimental data currently exists for this architecture. This paper establishes the formal theoretical and architectural foundation. Phase 1 experimental validation is the immediate next step.

1. Introduction

1.1 The Problem with Wireless Power Today

Wireless power transfer has been a subject of active research and commercial development for more than sixty years, yet the field has not produced a system capable of operating deterministically. Current commercial wireless power systems share a fundamental architectural limitation: none of them can guarantee delivery. They transmit energy into the environment and rely on proximity, orientation, or statistical averaging to achieve acceptable transfer rates.

This limitation is not a product of insufficient engineering effort. It is a consequence of the underlying architectural approach: existing wireless power systems are broadcast systems. They were not designed to route. Routing requires a formal model of the network, a defined path between source and destination, confirmation that delivery occurred, and a recovery mechanism when delivery fails. No existing wireless power system provides any of these four properties.

1.2 The Routing Reframe

The foundational insight of this work is that wireless energy delivery is a routing problem, not a transmission problem. Packet‑switched data networks solved this problem decades ago, and the conceptual framework they produced — addressed nodes, routing tables, QoS guarantees, topology‑aware path selection, failure detection and recovery — applies directly and powerfully to wireless energy delivery.

1.3 The Meridian Architecture

Meridian is a four‑layer deterministic wireless energy routing architecture:

  • Meridian Core (MC) — the foundational coordinate system defining node identity, spatial orientation, charge‑state vector representation, and deterministic timing primitives.
  • Meridian Flow Engine (MFE) — the energy‑flow logic layer converting charge‑state data into deterministic routing decisions.
  • Meridian Mesh Fabric (MMF) — the multi‑hop coordination layer maintaining routing tables, link‑state awareness, and neighbor‑graph topology.
  • Meridian Transmission Layer (MTL) — the physical execution layer controlling beam steering, RF emission shaping, safety gating, and closed‑loop delivery confirmation.

These four layers are vertically coupled and governed by five global invariants enforced continuously by Lume‑X: no uncontrolled discharge, no ambiguous routing, no non‑deterministic flow, no unsafe transmission events, and no mesh‑level oscillation.

1.5 Scope and Honest Limitations

This paper presents a formal theoretical and architectural framework. No experimental data exists at time of writing. Current development scale powers low‑power devices: sensors, wearables, IoT nodes, and microcontrollers. Claims about powering critical infrastructure are long‑horizon goals contingent on solving open efficiency and energy‑density problems.

2. Background and Related Work

2.1 Wireless Power Transfer

Microwave Power Transmission (MPT). William Brown at Raytheon demonstrated the first sustained microwave power link in the 1960s and 1970s, achieving approximately 54% rectenna conversion efficiency at fixed‑point operation. NASA’s Solar Power Satellite concept extended this work to orbital scale.

Resonant Inductive Coupling. Kurs et al. at MIT (2007) demonstrated mid‑range wireless power transfer using strongly coupled magnetic resonators, achieving approximately 40% efficiency over 2 meters. Range is fundamentally limited by resonator coil diameter.

Phased‑Array Beamforming. Phased‑array antenna technology is mature in radar, 5G massive MIMO, and satellite communications. Modern phased‑array systems at 28 GHz achieve beam widths of less than two degrees.

Commercial Systems. WiTricity, Ossia Cota, and Energous WattUp — none formally define determinism, none provide closed‑loop delivery confirmation, and none implement routing.

2.5 Identified Gaps Addressed by This Work

  1. No wireless power system implements formal routing. Meridian implements the full network protocol stack architecture.
  2. No wireless power system defines or enforces a formal determinism standard. Meridian defines Pmin/r/σ²/d.
  3. No wireless power system integrates a self‑healing control runtime. Meridian integrates Lume‑X with SHDCL.
  4. No wireless power system integrates ambient harvesting as a coordinated architectural layer.
  5. No wireless power system has a formal security threat model. Meridian integrates Guardian Security.

DAEH performs ambient energy harvesting and burst‑mode storage. Meridian provides the coordinate system, the flow logic, the mesh coordination, and the transmission governance layer.

5. Meridian Flow Engine (MFE)

5.2 Flow‑Selection Algorithm

Priority score P:

P = w₁ × (V_cap - V_burst)/(V_max - V_burst)   [charge surplus]
  + w₂ × (destination_priority)                  [urgency]
  + w₃ × (link_quality)                          [reliability]
  + w₄ × (1 - mesh_load)                         [congestion]

Default weights: w1 = 0.4, w2 = 0.3, w3 = 0.2, w4 = 0.1.

9. Mesh Routing Integration (DRMA)

9.2 Why Short Hops Beat Long Hops

At 2.4 GHz, FSPL increases by 6 dB every time distance doubles. Breaking a 4‑meter path into four 1‑meter hops saves approximately 12 dB in path loss per hop but incurs per‑hop conversion losses (30–52% round‑trip efficiency). Break‑even occurs at approximately 2–3 meters.

10. Wireless Transmission Integration (DWER)

10.2 The Determinism Standard

Deterministic delivery: for a given transmitter configuration and receiver position, the system guarantees Pmin (mW) within spatial uncertainty radius r (cm), with variance σ² below threshold, across N ≥ 20 repeated trials under identical conditions, at distance d (m).
