Epilogue
What Comes Next

The eight chapters of this book have made a complete argument. Let me state it plainly before stepping back.

Meridian is a routing architecture for energy. It is built on the same principles as the data internet — addressed nodes, routing tables, quality‑of‑service guarantees, delivery confirmation — and applies them to a physical resource. It is formally specified, safety‑proven, and deployable in real buildings. It scales to a global Energy Internet. It routes data and energy simultaneously through a unified fabric. It defends itself through Guardian Security. It is one instance of Deterministic Infrastructure. And in the full stack vision, it extends upward to eliminate the physical transmission grid entirely.

Phase 1: The Experimental Foundation

Nothing in this book has been experimentally validated. The first step is Phase 1 validation of the room‑scale Meridian architecture: build the hardware, run the control software, measure the four determinism metrics (P_min, r, σ², d), and honestly report whether the claims hold up in physical reality.

The Regulatory Path

Meridian is designed to operate within FCC Part 15 at 60 GHz for initial deployment. A formal safety submission backed by Phase 1–3 experimental data demonstrating BSL effectiveness is the mechanism. Guardian Security’s formal threat model is a component of the regulatory submission.

The BIM Integration Partnership

The Meridian BIM plugin requires cooperation with Autodesk (Revit) and Bentley (OpenBuildings). The specification exists; the partnership discussions can begin in parallel with experimental work.

The Receiver Partnership Problem

Full realization of the SSD model requires consumer electronics manufacturers to embed UEDM receiver hardware. The resolution: target purpose‑built devices first (IoT sensors, medical monitors, industrial equipment), then consumer electronics once infrastructure is deployed.

The Broader Arc

Four more DI domains — transportation (DMI), manufacturing (DPI), emergency coordination (DRI), and ambient computation (DII) — will produce their own instances. The Meridian papers are the template. The Lume ecosystem is the substrate. The general theory is the guide.

The work ahead is long. The foundation is now laid.

A 3D volumetric representation of a building encoded as typed voxels (SOLID, FLOOR_SAFE, FLOOR_OCCUPIED, VOID_STAIRCASE, VOID_ATRIUM, VOID_SHAFT, GATEWAY_ZONE) used as hard routing constraints.
BSL (Biological Safety Layer)
Hardware‑enforced safety mechanism preventing beam activation when biological presence is detected. Independent of the software stack; cannot be bypassed by software attacks.
Burst‑Mode Delivery
Energy accumulated in supercapacitors and released in high‑power, short‑duration RF bursts, enabling phased‑array beam steering for energy delivery.
Ceiling‑Plane Routing
Standard deployment posture: relay nodes at ceiling height, beam paths in FLOOR_SAFE voxels for all non‑final hops. Geometric separation from the occupancy zone.
DAEH (Deterministic Ambient Energy Harvesting)
Subsystem for harvesting ambient energy (solar, RF, thermal, kinetic) and managing supercapacitor charge state.
DAIGS
Multi‑agent cognition framework. A DI instance in the computation domain.

Autonomous physical systems defined by four properties: verified identity, explicitly routed resources, invariant‑enforced homeostasis, and organism‑like self‑maintenance.
DRMA (Distributed Relay Mesh Architecture)
Subsystem maintaining mesh topology, computing routes, and coordinating TDMA schedules.
DWER (Deterministic Wireless Energy Routing)
Point‑to‑point directed beam delivery at the final hop.

Four‑layer deterministic wireless energy routing architecture: MC, MFE, MMF, MTL.
MFE (Meridian Flow Engine)

Self‑Sustaining Device (SSD)
UEDM participant requiring no battery and no separate radio — receives both power and data from the mesh.

Continuous monitoring of invariant domains with pre‑specified recovery sequences.
