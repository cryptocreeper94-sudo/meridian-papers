11 Related Work

11.1 Solar Power Satellite and Space‑Based Solar Power

The SPS concept, first formalized by Peter Glaser in 1968 [80] and developed into the NASA/DOE 1979 reference design [81], proposed harvesting solar energy in geostationary orbit and beaming it to Earth via 2.45 GHz. End‑to‑end efficiency was approximately 7–13%. This paper’s Tier 1 architecture is the terrestrial analog — retaining the technically validated elements (2.45 GHz, pilot‑tone, rectenna, Fresnel analysis) while eliminating orbital manufacturing requirements.

Recent programs — JAXA’s SSPS [82], ESA’s SOLARIS [83], and Caltech’s SSPP demonstration [84] — have advanced the hardware technology base, particularly solid‑state transmitter efficiency and lightweight deployable receivers.

11.2 Terrestrial Microwave Power Transmission Demonstrations

Milestones include Brown’s 1964 microwave‑powered helicopter [85]; the 1975 Goldstone demonstration of 30 kW at 54% DC‑to‑DC efficiency over 1.6 km [86]; the 2008 LaserMotive 1 km optical demonstration [87]; PowerLight Technologies’ 400 W at 300m [88]; and Emrod’s multi‑km trials [89]. None operate at the power levels or distances proposed for Tier 1, but they establish physical feasibility.

11.3 Fusion Energy and Deterministic Control

Commercial fusion development has accelerated since 2020 (Commonwealth Fusion Systems, TAE Technologies, Helion Energy, General Fusion, ITER). The deterministic control problem is the subject of the companion Canon² series and is not surveyed in detail here.

11.4 Grid Resilience and Wireless Energy

Grid vulnerability to geomagnetic disturbance [90, 91], economic outage costs [92], and substation security [93] collectively motivate the resilience argument. The wireless architecture is the architectural response to the vulnerabilities they document.

12 Limitations and Honest Boundaries

The efficiency penalty is real and large. At current technology levels, the wireless architecture delivers approximately 15% of fusion thermal energy to the device vs. ~30% for the wired grid. This 2× penalty cannot be fully eliminated by technology improvement. The decision to build wireless requires explicit justification in each deployment context.

No demonstration at scale exists. The largest terrestrial demonstrations deliver hundreds of watts over hundreds of meters. Tier 1 proposes megawatts over kilometers — four orders of magnitude in power and one in distance. Intermediate‑scale demonstrations (100 kW over 1 km; 1 MW over 3 km) are necessary for validation.

Fusion energy is not yet commercially available. The architecture is designed for the world in which fusion is commercial — it cannot be built today. This paper is a specification for that world.

The regulatory pathway is long and uncertain. The FCC, FAA, and state commissions all have jurisdiction. No existing framework directly addresses this. Developing the framework through experimental licensing and rulemakings will take a decade or more.

Pilot‑tone spoofing (T13) is a non‑trivial threat. The retrodirective mechanism prevents software attacks but is vulnerable to physical pilot‑tone capture and replication. Physical security of the T1‑RX pilot transmitter is the primary physical security requirement. The threat is categorized in Guardian Security T13 but does not yet have a complete formal defense specification.

13 Conclusion

The argument of this paper is simple, even if the engineering is not: a world with fusion energy and a complete wireless routing architecture for energy delivery does not need transmission towers, high‑voltage cables, or substations. The energy can travel through air, addressed and routed, from plasma to device.

The argument has two parts. The first is that the physics permits it. Fresnel‑regime microwave power beaming at 2.45 GHz, with 10–20m apertures, achieves 80–87% collection efficiency at district distances. Retrodirective pilot‑tone locking provides a physically robust safety mechanism that cannot be bypassed by software attacks. The physics is not speculative.

The second is that the architecture connects. The Tier 1 beam layer interfaces cleanly with the Tier 2 Meridian mesh at the DC bus. The fusion control system provides a deterministically governed generation source. The Trust Layer, Lume‑X, and Guardian Security operate across all three layers.

The efficiency penalty is real. The regulatory path is long. The fusion source is not yet commercially available. These are honest limitations stated without minimization.

The target is worth building toward. A global energy system in which energy is routed wirelessly from distributed fusion hubs to the districts they serve, then through a self‑healing building mesh to every device — without a single transmission tower or high‑voltage cable — is a genuinely better infrastructure architecture. It is more resilient, more flexible, more equitable in deployment, and more compatible with the autonomous physical world that the rest of the Meridian series describes.

The routed world does not stop at the building boundary.

Appendix B — Retrodirective Pilot‑Tone Safety Protocol

T1-RX pilot tone: f_pilot = f_beam + Δf (Δf = 1 MHz for 2.45 GHz beam)
Pilot power: 1W ERP from phase-reference element at T1-RX center
Pilot source power: independent battery (≥30 day capacity) or dedicated solar panel
Pilot receive threshold at T1-TX: -60 dBm (20 dB margin above noise)

T1-TX hardware safety gate:
  POWER_AMPLIFIER_ENABLE = pilot_received AND (pilot_power > threshold)
    AND intruder_clear AND regulatory_mode_active
  Loss of pilot → PA_ENABLE = FALSE within 1 hardware clock cycle
  Time from pilot loss to beam off: ≤ 100 μs (hardware gate, no software path)

  Pilot carrier modulated with 64-bit rolling code via ECDH key exchange
  T1-TX verifies rolling code; unkeyed source cannot produce valid code
  Key rotation: every 24 hours via Trust Layer key exchange
