5 Fusion Integration: Deterministic Generation to Deterministic Routing

5.1 The Deterministic Fusion Control Framework

The companion Canon² series applies the core Lume‑X invariant enforcement model to magnetic confinement fusion plasma. Four primary invariants characterize the plasma state:

INV‑F1 (Energy Confinement): τE ≥ τE,min — energy confinement time at or above the minimum for sustained ignition. Violation triggers plasma heating adjustment.

INV‑F2 (Beta Limit): β ≤ βmax — plasma beta below the MHD stability limit. Violation triggers magnetic field adjustment or plasma density reduction.

INV‑F3 (Greenwald Density): ne ≤ nG — electron density below the Greenwald density limit. Violation triggers fueling rate reduction.

INV‑F4 (Energy Gain): Q ≥ 1 — energy multiplication factor at or above breakeven. The operational invariant: net energy production only when Q ≥ 1.

Lume‑X enforces these invariants at the plasma control timescale (1–10 ms for MHD instabilities), consistent with the Lume‑X cycle time specification.

5.2 Output Modulation

A fusion reactor governed by the framework can modulate output within [Pmin_fusion, Pmax_fusion] by adjusting fueling rate and auxiliary heating power. Plasma thermal response time: 1–10 seconds. The T1 control cycle (100ms) is faster than the plasma modulation response. Demand‑following operates across three timescales:

  • Short‑term (<1s): Buffered by district rectenna’s local supercapacitor bank and short‑term battery.
  • Medium‑term (1–10s): Tracked by fusion output modulation response.
  • Long‑term (>10s): Handled by fusion output modulation with full settling.

6 Tier 1 Safety Architecture at Scale

6.1 The Categorically Different Safety Problem

The Meridian BSL is designed for milliwatt‑to‑watt beams over 1–10 meters in a building interior. None of its assumptions hold for megawatt beams over kilometers of open airspace.

A 10 MW beam at 2.45 GHz from a 10m aperture produces ~130 kW/m² at the aperture face and ~10–30 kW/m² at the receiver 5 km away — 1,000–3,000× above the ICNIRP public reference level of 10 W/m². The Tier 1 safety architecture must be a prevention system, not a detection‑and‑response system.

6.2 Retrodirective Pilot‑Tone Locking

The central safety element, originally developed for the NASA Solar Power Satellite reference design:

  1. The T1‑RX transmits a low‑power pilot tone at fbeam + Δf (e.g., 2.4502 GHz) from a phase‑reference antenna at the receiver array center.
  2. The T1‑TX receives the pilot, computes the precise direction, and conjugates the beam phase to converge at the pilot source location.
  3. The T1‑TX emits power only when the pilot is actively received above a minimum threshold. Loss of pilot = beam stops — hardware‑enforced via a hardware AND gate on the PA chain. No software command required.
  4. The pilot transmitter runs on an independent power source (dedicated battery ≥30 days or standalone solar panel), ensuring main power loss at the receiver does not cause beam loss.

Safety guarantee: The beam is physically impossible to redirect to an unintended receiver — the transmitter only knows the direction from which the pilot arrives. Redirecting requires physically capturing the pilot transmitter, a physical attack requiring site access.

6.4 Intruder Detection

Three independent systems operate in parallel:

  • Radar monitoring: X‑band (10 GHz) surveillance radar monitoring the beam corridor for objects >0.1 m² RCS. Detection triggers power reduction to 1% within 50ms, shutoff within 100ms.
  • ADS‑B integration: Aircraft transponder data ingested; automatic shutoff when any trajectory is predicted to intersect the exclusion corridor within 60 seconds.
  • Optical/thermal camera: Camera array along beam path for ground‑level incursion detection. Human presence triggers immediate shutoff.

All three connect to beam shutoff hardware via independent hardwired channels. Any single event triggers cutoff. Re‑activation requires all three systems clear plus human operator confirmation. Re‑activation is not automatic.

6.5 Comparison with Existing High‑Power RF Infrastructure

Existing precedents include ATC radar (500 kW peak at 2.7–2.9 GHz), Cold War troposcatter links (10–100 kW over 100–300 km), and satellite uplink earth stations (up to 75 kW EIRP continuous). The Tier 1 architecture is architecturally similar, adapted for terrestrial power beaming geometry.

7.2 The Efficiency Counterargument

The relevant comparison is total system cost per delivered watt‑year, including capital, operating, and externalized costs. Three factors shift this comparison:

  • Capital cost elimination: The wired grid charges $4 trillion of T&D infrastructure. The wireless architecture’s capital cost is phased array antennas, rectennas, and Meridian nodes — solid‑state electronics with 20–30 year lifetimes and no civil infrastructure.
  • Fuel cost irrelevance: With fusion, fuel cost is near‑zero. The efficiency penalty means slightly more generation capacity, not fuel exhaustion.
  • Resilience valuation: U.S. grid outage costs are estimated at $150 billion per year (Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory). A wireless architecture with no physical plant to fail eliminates this cost class.

7.3 Efficiency Improvement Trajectories

With projected 2030 technology levels — rectenna 88–92%, device receiver 80–85%, 20m apertures for 83% collection, 3‑hop average for 86% mesh efficiency — projected thermal‑to‑device efficiency reaches approximately 21–24%. The gap narrows but does not close on efficiency alone. The architecture does not need to win on efficiency — it needs to win on total system cost, resilience, deployment speed, and flexibility.
